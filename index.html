The Story.

Didn't plan it.
Couldn't stop.
1990
—
2000
How It Started

I grew up in Mexico, Missouri. Nine thousand people, one stoplight, and a school library with a computer that could render 3D graphics. Each frame took seven minutes. I watched every one of them. Nobody told me this was a strange way to spend an afternoon, so I kept doing it.

2000
—
2020
The Work

Twenty years. GE Healthcare. Blue Cross Blue Shield. TransUnion. Google. Systems used by hundreds of thousands of people who never knew my name and didn't need to. I was good at it. I also made music and art the entire time — privately, for no audience. Not as a side project. Just because I couldn't stop.

Thirty
—
years
What I Was Managing

For thirty years I was on daily medication for chronic pain. It worked, mostly. I showed up. The work was good. When I look back at what I made during those years, I'm still proud of it. I don't spend a lot of time wondering what it would have been otherwise.

2022
—
2024
The Change

I stopped the medication. Started swimming every morning — an hour, then longer. Pilates. Weights. A PT pool. Lost thirty-five pounds. The fog I didn't know I was in lifted gradually, month by month. I kept waiting for the other shoe. It didn't drop.

2026
—
Now
Now

I opened the studio. Started sharing the music and art I'd been making privately for years. Built this site. Got a cat named Hex. That's more or less everything.

The Person

The Human.

I've always made more than one kind of thing.
I used to apologize for that.
I stopped.

My name is Mikel Rosenthal. I go by PBK. I live in Chicago Heights with my husband Brandon and our black cat Hex — a rescue who showed up three Halloweens ago and clearly had somewhere to be.

I've been a designer for twenty-five years. I also make music in single takes, have been doing motion art since 1999, do voiceover work, and build sites from scratch. I'm learning Portuguese. I'm mentioning all of this not to impress you but because there's no shorter version. They're all the same thing. I just haven't found the word for it yet.

The music is unrepeatable by design — one pass, live-mastered, whatever happened in the room is what it is. The client work is the opposite of that. But the instinct underneath is the same: make something real, make it once, make it right.

I spent thirty years managing chronic pain and came out the other side two years ago. That changed things in ways I'm still noticing.

I
No Walls

I've never been able to keep the disciplines separate. A design decision is a musical decision is an architectural decision. They're different surfaces for the same question. I stopped trying to sort them into boxes sometime around 2005.

II
The Long Clearing

For three decades the work ran on a compromised engine — daily medication that worked, but at a cost I didn't fully understand until I stopped. Two years ago I stopped. I invested instead in movement: yoga, Pilates, weight training, swimming every morning. Lost thirty-five pounds. Lost the fog. Every discipline I have — design, music, language, code, art, voice — now connects at a speed and depth I didn't know I was capable of. I had to get out of the way to find out what was there.

III
On Purpose

If it doesn't serve something beyond the screen — a person, a community, a question worth asking — it doesn't leave the studio.

Observations

The Thinking.

01
On
Confusion

I've never met a confusing interface that was designed to be confusing. The confusion is always the result of someone who stopped caring about the person on the other end. That's it. That's the whole diagnosis.

02
On
Systems

The best design system I ever built was the one where, six months later, nobody on the team could tell which components I'd added versus which had always been there. That's what good infrastructure feels like. It disappears.

03
On
Stillness

I thought for a long time that being productive was the point. I was wrong about that. Being clear is the point. It took me thirty years and a lot of swimming to learn the difference. Most mornings I can tell within ten minutes which one I'm operating from.

04
On
Attention

White space isn't decoration. It's respect. Every element you add to a page costs the person looking at it something — time, attention, goodwill. Most sites are in debt before anyone reads a word. I try to work from a surplus.

05
On
AI

I use AI constantly. It's remarkable at completing the sentence you've already started. What it can't do is start a sentence that surprises even the person writing it. That's the whole game, actually — and it's still a human one.

06
On Being
Wrong

The older I get, the more comfortable I am saying I don't know. Not as a hedge. As an honest answer. The most interesting things are genuinely uncertain. The people who are certain about everything are usually the most lost. I've been one of them. I prefer this.
